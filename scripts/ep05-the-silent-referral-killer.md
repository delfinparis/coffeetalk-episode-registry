## COLD OPEN (0:00 – ~0:45)

**D.J.:** (setting down coffee, looking at camera) 83 percent.

**TIM:** 83% of... what?

**D.J.:** That's how many potential clients will never call you. Not because they don't want to. Because they looked you up first.

**TIM:** Looked you up — and then what?

**D.J.:** Didn't like what they found. Or found nothing at all.

**TIM:** (leaning in) We're talking about Google.

**D.J.:** I'm D.J. Paris, that's Tim Littleton — welcome to Coffee Talk. Today we're talking about the thing that's quietly killing your referrals before they ever reach your phone. And by the end of this, you're going to fix it. For free.

## SEGMENT 1: THE PROBLEM (~0:45 – ~3:30)

**TIM:** Okay. 83 percent won't call because of what they found — or didn't find — on Google. Back that up.

**D.J.:** Here's the full picture. 65% of sellers find their agent through a referral. Agents know this. They celebrate it. "My whole business is word of mouth." And they're proud of that — as they should be.

**TIM:** That sounds like a great problem to have.

**D.J.:** It is. Until you understand what happens between the referral and the actual phone call. Somebody says, "Hey, you should call my agent — her name is Sarah, she's amazing." And the person nods, pulls out their phone, and before they ever dial — they Google Sarah.

**TIM:** Of course they do!

**D.J.:** Of course. BrightLocal tracked this in 2024. 81% of consumers check Google reviews before making a decision. On anything — a restaurant, a plumber, an agent. The referral is just the introduction.

**TIM:** So the referral doesn't close the deal. Google does.

**D.J.:** Google is the audition. The referral just gets you in the room. And here's where it gets uncomfortable: an expert estimate widely circulated in real estate SEO circles puts roughly 80% of agents as having no real Google Business Profile — either nothing, or an auto-generated ghost listing with outdated information they've never touched.

**TIM:** Wait. 80% have basically nothing?

**D.J.:** Nothing. Or worse than nothing. So your client refers you. Their friend Googles your name. They find a stub listing. Maybe a wrong phone number. Four reviews — the most recent one from two years ago. They close the tab.

**TIM:** And you never knew the referral even happened.

**D.J.:** You never knew. That's the part that should keep agents up at night. It's not a call that went to voicemail. It's a decision made before you ever had a chance to open your mouth. A silent no.

**TIM:** Silent referral killer.

**D.J.:** And it gets more specific. Studies show 83% of clients will avoid an agent with a rating below four stars. Not "prefer not to." Will avoid. So if you've got a 3.8 and four old reviews — you're losing referrals you didn't know you had.

**TIM:** Okay. That's painful. So why isn't everyone fixing this?

**D.J.:** That's exactly where it gets interesting.

---

## SEGMENT 2: WHY IT HAPPENS (~3:30 – ~6:30)

**D.J.:** Three reasons. First one — agents don't know what they look like on Google. They've never searched themselves.

**TIM:** I'll be honest. I haven't done it recently. Maybe like a couple of times.

**D.J.:** Most agents haven't. And it's not laziness — it just doesn't occur to you. You're not the one Googling you. Your clients are. So there's this massive blind spot. You're working relationships, serving clients, closing deals — and meanwhile, attached to your name, there's a digital storefront you've never walked into.

**TIM:** The lights are off and the sign fell down.

**D.J.:** Exactly that. Second reason: agents think Google is a lead generation tool — something for finding strangers, not serving people who are already coming to you.

**TIM:** Like it's a billboard, not a front door.

**D.J.:** Right framing. Your Google profile isn't advertising. It's the last thing someone checks before they decide to pick up the phone. That's a fundamentally different job.

**TIM:** And the third reason?

**D.J.:** They collected a handful of reviews at some point and stopped asking. So the last review is two years old. Which sends its own signal — is this person still active? Still any good?

**TIM:** Recency matters. Even a good review that's old starts to feel stale.

**D.J.:** Okay — here's where we stop talking and you do something. Right now. If you're watching live — do this while we talk. Catching the replay — hit pause. Either way: open Google, search your own name plus your city. "Sarah Johnson Chicago." "Mike Torres Dallas." Whatever your name and market are.

**TIM:** Your name. Your city. Go.

**D.J.:** Look at what comes up. How many reviews do you have? What's your star rating? Is there even a profile, or just a stub that Google auto-generated from whatever it scraped from the internet?

**TIM:** (sips coffee, waits) I'm genuinely a little nervous doing this.

**D.J.:** (smiles) Good. That feeling is information. Write down that review number — whatever it is. Zero, three, fifteen, doesn't matter. We're going to use it in a few minutes.

**TIM:** The system for going from that number to something that actually works for you — that's next.

---

## SEGMENT 3: THE SYSTEM/SOLUTION (~6:30 – ~11:00)

**D.J.:** The framework is called The Google Gut Check. Three steps. None of them are expensive. Together they turn your digital storefront from a ghost town into the thing that actually finishes your referrals.

**TIM:** Three steps. What's step one?

**D.J.:** Step one is The Self-Search — which you just did. The mission is knowing your baseline. Your star rating. Your review count. And critically — whether you have a real Google Business Profile or just an auto-generated stub that Google built from whatever data it could find. Because those stubs can have your old brokerage address. An old phone number. Sometimes a photo that isn't you. And you never set it up — Google just populated it. You'd never know unless you looked.

**TIM:** So some agents have been operating with a Google listing they didn't create, with wrong info, wondering why the inbound is slow.

**D.J.:** For years. Step one is knowing the truth.

**TIM:** Okay. What does step two look like?

**D.J.:** Step two is The Profile Patch. This is your 20-minute fix, and it's completely free. Go to Google Business Profile — just search that phrase, it'll come up — and claim your listing. Then fill in everything: your professional photo, your current brokerage, your service areas, your direct phone number, your website. And write a bio that actually sounds like a human wrote it. Not a legal disclaimer. Not "licensed real estate professional specializing in buyer and seller representation." Something like — "I've helped over 80 families buy and sell homes on the north side of Chicago. I'm probably going to become your go-to call for anything house-related."

**TIM:** (laughs) That actually sounds like a person.

**D.J.:** That's the point. The common mistake is rushing the patch and leaving half the fields blank. Incomplete profiles actually signal lower credibility to Google's algorithm — you rank lower in local search, and clients notice the emptiness.

**TIM:** So do it once and do it right.

**D.J.:** Twenty minutes. One time. Works for you 24 hours a day after that.

**TIM:** And step three — I'm guessing this is where the review count we wrote down comes in.

**D.J.:** That's exactly where it comes in. Step three is The Review Request. This is where most agents have the biggest gap and the most upside — because most never ask. The deal closes, the handshake happens, and then... nothing. No ask.

**TIM:** And the client would have written a review. They liked you.

**D.J.:** Research consistently shows that satisfied clients will leave a review when asked directly and promptly — within a week of closing. After that, life takes over. The moment passes. So the system is simple: within 72 hours of every single closing, you send one message. Here it is.

**TIM:** Give it to them.

**D.J.:** "Hey [Name] — working with you was genuinely one of the best parts of my year. If you have two minutes, a Google review would mean the world to me — it helps families like yours find me when they need help. Here's the direct link: [your Google review link]." That's the whole message.

**TIM:** The link is the key part, right? Remove the friction.

**D.J.:** People don't know how to find your Google profile and leave a review. They need one tap to a direct URL. Without the link, half of them intend to do it and never figure out how.

**TIM:** How do you get that direct link?

**D.J.:** Inside your Google Business Profile dashboard, there's a "Share review form" button. One click, and Google generates a link you can paste into any text or email. Bookmark it. Put it in your phone. Use it every single closing.

**TIM:** (pauses) If you just Googled yourself a few minutes ago and found basically nothing — tell us what you found in the comments. You are not alone in this. I promise.

**D.J.:** And when you send that first review request this week — come back and tell us. We want to see those numbers change.
