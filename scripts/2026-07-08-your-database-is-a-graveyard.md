## SCRIPT

### COLD OPEN (0:00 – ~0:45)

**D.J.:** *(setting the coffee down, straight to camera)* One in five. *(beat)*

That's how many of your deals should come from someone you've already sold a house to. One in five, for the average agent. For the agent who does this right, it can be more than half. *(sips coffee)* So be honest with yourself. When's the last time you actually talked to a past client? Not liked their photo. Talked to them.

I'm D.J. Paris. Today we walk through your database together, and I show you the graveyard you've been building. Stay with me. This one pays for itself before lunch.

### SEGMENT 1: THE PROBLEM (~0:45 – ~3:30)

**D.J.:** Here's the real number. The National Association of REALTORS, their 2025 Member Profile, agents reporting their own business. The typical agent gets one in five of their deals from repeat clients. People they've already sold a home to. *(beat)*

And that's before referrals. Another twenty-one percent of the average agent's business comes from past clients sending friends their way. Two separate streams. Both pouring out of the same list you're ignoring.

Now you're probably thinking one in five sounds fine. That's the trap. Because that same report says agents who've been at this sixteen years or longer? Four in ten of them say repeat business alone is more than half of everything they do. *(leans in)* More than half. No cold calling. No buying leads. No dancing on TikTok at seven in the morning.

So what separates the one-in-five agent from the more-than-half agent? *(beat)* It's not talent. It's not their market. It's not luck. It's that one of them treats past clients like a business, and the other treats them like a memory.

Here's the picture I can't shake. You've got hundreds of names in your phone. Every one of them trusted you with the biggest purchase of their life. Then closing day comes, you hand over the keys, and *(pause)* silence. Inman ran a piece just this month. The headline was "You didn't lose that client to a better agent. You lost them to silence."

That's the graveyard. Rows of headstones, every one a name you used to know. And you never visit.

### SEGMENT 2: WHY IT HAPPENS (~3:30 – ~6:30)

**D.J.:** So why does this happen? *(beat)* I don't think you're lazy. I think three things work against you, and you'll recognize all three.

Number one. It feels weird. You sold Karen a house two years ago, and reaching out now feels like you want something. Like you're about to hit her with "know anybody looking to sell?" So you don't reach out at all, because the only version you can picture is the gross one.

Number two. There's no system, so it's always later. You'll get to your database when things slow down. But things never slow down. And later is where past clients go to die.

Number three, the sneaky one. It doesn't hurt today. Ignoring your list has zero consequences this morning. Nobody calls to yell at you. The pain is invisible, and it's a year away, so your brain files it under someday. *(sips coffee)*

Real quick, tell me in the comments. Which one's you? One, two, or three? No judgment. I've been all three.

Alright, now do one thing for me. Think of one past client. Not a name off a list. Someone you genuinely liked. Someone you'd actually grab coffee with. Their name just popped into your head, didn't it? *(beat)* Don't type it, don't say it out loud. Just hold onto that person. Because in a few minutes, you and I are going to do something with them. Something small. Something that works.

This is fixable. It's not hard. It's just never been built. Let me build it with you.

### SEGMENT 3: THE SYSTEM/SOLUTION (~6:30 – ~11:00)

**D.J.:** Here's the system. Three moves. I call it The Dig. Because we're going into the graveyard, and we're bringing people back. Exhume, Tier, Touch.

Move one. Exhume. Get every past client into one single place. Right now they're scattered. Some in your email, some in your phone, a few on a sticky note that's been on your monitor since 2023. That's not a database. That's a crime scene. So you pull them into one list, and it doesn't matter if it's a fancy CRM or a free spreadsheet. One list, one place, everyone who ever paid you a commission or sent you a referral. That list is your actual business.

That name you're holding? They're on this list. Almost time. Stay with me.

Move two. Tier. Not everybody gets the same attention, and pretending they do is why people quit after a month. So you sort the list into three groups. Your A-list is the twenty or thirty people who love you and would refer you tomorrow if you gave them a reason. Your B-list is everybody else you've closed. Your C-list is the loose connections. The A-list is the whole game. Those twenty or thirty people are your entire year if you treat them right.

Quick gut check, and actually do this. How many past clients are sitting in your phone right now? Ballpark it, drop the number in the comments. One number. *(glances off-camera)* Yeah, I'm already seeing them roll in. And every one of those is somebody you could talk to this week.

Move three. Touch. And this is the one everybody butchers. A touch is not "just checking in." *(shaking head)* "Just checking in" is the most useless sentence in real estate. A real touch gives them something before you ask for anything. Here's your menu, steal any of these. One, the value text. "Your place is probably worth around this now, thought you'd want to know." Two, the neighbor sold. "A house just went on your street for this, wild, right?" Three, the anniversary. "One year ago today you got your keys. Hope it still feels like home."

Every one of those gives before it asks. And you run it on a schedule. Your A-list, four times a year, minimum. Once a quarter. Anybody can find four moments a year to be useful to someone who already likes them. Here's why the gift matters. The second you lead with "know anybody selling," you're a telemarketer, and people tune that out. But nobody has ever been annoyed by their agent telling them what their house is worth. *(beat)* Lead with the gift. The ask takes care of itself.

### SEGMENT 4: THE HARD OBJECTION (~11:00 – ~12:45)

**D.J.:** Now some of you have been down this road, and I can hear it, because I've heard it a hundred times. *(leaning in)* "D.J., I tried this. I signed up for a CRM, I sent a newsletter for two months, and I got nothing. It doesn't work." *(beat)*

I believe you. And here's why it didn't work. Two reasons. One, a newsletter is a broadcast, not a touch. It talks at four hundred people. A touch talks to one person about their house. Totally different animal. Two, you gave it two months. Repeat and referral business doesn't pay back in a season. It compounds. The agent with half their business walking back through the door didn't get there in a spring. They got there by touching the same A-list, four times a year, for years.

So here's your one move. Don't rebuild a newsletter. Take your A-list, and this week, send five of them one personal message about their own home. Not four hundred. Five. That's the test that actually works.

### SEGMENT 5: THE MATH/MOTIVATION (~12:45 – ~14:15)

**D.J.:** Let me put the money on the table, because here's where it stops being a nice idea. Say you close ten deals this year. If you're the average agent, two of those already come from repeat clients, whether you try or not. *(beat)* Now picture letting the rest go cold. Every past client you ignore is somebody who's going to buy or sell again, or knows three people who will. And when that day comes, they won't remember you. They'll Google somebody. Or worse, another agent was texting them the whole time you stayed silent.

That's not a missed opportunity. That's you handing your next commission to the agent who did the work you skipped. The graveyard isn't just quiet. It's expensive. Every headstone in there is a check you're mailing to your competition.

### CLOSE (~14:15 – ~15:00)

**D.J.:** Okay. Exhume, Tier, Touch. That's The Dig. *(beat)*

That past client you've been holding? Here's exactly what you do before you close this stream. Open your texts. Send this. "Hey [Name], a place just sold on your street. Yours is probably worth around [number] now. No reason, just thought you'd want to know." Send it. *(beat)*

And you're not bad at follow-up. That was the old story, and it let you off the hook for years. You just gave a past client something useful. That's not follow-up. That's you running the one lead source that compounds while you sleep. Those agents don't chase business. It comes looking for them.

*(raises coffee)* Go dig one grave today. See you next week.

**END**
