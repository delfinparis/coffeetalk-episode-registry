## COLD OPEN (0:00 – ~0:45)

D.J.: *(setting the coffee down, straight to camera)* Nearly one in seven. *(beat)* That's how many home sale agreements fell apart last month instead of closing. Not fell apart in negotiation. Fell apart after everybody already celebrated.

I'm D.J. Paris. And here's the part that should scare you.

Ask agents how often this happens to them, and they'll give you a number less than half that big. So either you're twice as good as the entire market, or you're not counting the ones you lost.

Stay with me. We're fixing the thirty days nobody works.

## SEGMENT 1: THE PROBLEM (~0:45 – ~3:30)

D.J.: Here's the actual number. In May, 13.6 percent of home sale agreements in this country fell through. That's Redfin's June report, and it isn't a survey. Those are real deals that went under contract and then died before the closing table.

*(leans in)*

You probably heard that and thought "that can't be right, my deals close." *(beat)* Yeah. Every agent thinks that.

And you might figure this is some brand new panic thing. It isn't. That rate has barely moved in two straight years. Somewhere between thirteen and fourteen out of every hundred. Every single month.

This isn't a spike. It's the water you've been swimming in.

Now here's where it gets personal. Redfin ranked the fifty biggest metros. Atlanta came in worst at 18.8 percent. Then Fort Worth, Jacksonville, San Antonio, Orlando, all sitting right around eighteen.

*(sips coffee)*

Look at that list for a second. Those are the boom markets. Those are the places where a whole generation of agents got trained during the years when a signed contract basically was a closing.

That's the trap. If you learned this business between 2020 and 2022, you learned that the signature is the finish line. Offer accepted, you fire off the champagne text to your client, and the file closes itself.

That habit is quietly costing you deals right now.

Because think about what actually happens the day you go under contract. You exhale. You update your pipeline. You go back to prospecting, because that's what everybody told you to do. And for the next month, your buyer sits alone with the biggest financial decision of their life, and the only voices in their ear belong to their brother in law and the internet.

Nobody's holding the deal. Everybody assumes somebody else is.

And here's what I want you to sit with. The deal you lose in escrow is the most expensive deal you will ever lose. You already did the entire job. You just don't get paid for it.

That's the thirty days we're fixing today.

## SEGMENT 2: WHY IT HAPPENS (~3:30 – ~6:30)

D.J.: So why is this hitting good agents, in a market that's actually seeing buyers come back?

*(beat)*

Three reasons.

First one is the market itself. Redfin's own read is that buyers now have somewhere else to go. When there were three houses for sale in the whole town, backing out was terrifying. Now your buyer gets cold feet on Tuesday and finds two more houses they like by Thursday. Walking away used to cost them the house. Now it costs them a weekend.

Second reason, and this one stings a little. You don't think it's happening to you.

NAR asked agents in June what share of their contracts terminated over the previous three months. Agents said six percent. The transaction data says nearly fourteen. *(beat)* Now, those two numbers measure slightly different things, and I'm not going to pretend otherwise. But not by that much. We remember our closings in high definition. We file the collapses under "that one was weird."

*(sets the coffee down)*

Alright. Before I give you the fix, do something for me.

Think of one deal you have under contract right now. Just one. If you've got four going, pick the one that tightens your stomach a little when it crosses your mind. You know exactly which one it is. Don't type the address, don't say the name out loud. Just hold it.

*(beat)*

Got it? Good. Hold on to that one, because before we sign off I'm telling you exactly what to do with it tonight.

Actually, do type one thing. Not the address. Just the number of deals you've got pending right now. One number in the comments. I want to see what this room is carrying.

*(beat)*

Third reason. And honestly, this is the real one.

Nobody owns the middle.

Ask yourself who is genuinely responsible for your buyer's confidence between the day they sign and the day they close. *(sips coffee)* The lender thinks you've got it. You think the lender's got it. The attorney is handling the attorney part. Title is handling title. And your buyer, the one human being who can blow this entire thing up, is sitting at home at eleven at night reading about a recession.

Everybody's doing their job. Nobody's doing that job.

And look, it isn't that you don't care. You care a lot. You just never got handed a system for the part of the deal where nothing is technically wrong yet.

That's what's coming next.

## SEGMENT 3: THE SYSTEM/SOLUTION (~6:30 – ~11:00)

D.J.: The system's called the Thirty-Day Grip.

Thirty days, because NAR says contracts still typically close in about thirty days. Grip, because the whole point is you don't let go at the signature.

Three parts.

**Part one. The Day Zero Call.**

Within twenty four hours of mutual acceptance, you call your client. Not a text. A call. And on that call you do the one thing almost nobody does. You tell them exactly how the next month is going to feel.

You say: here's the calendar, and here are the three moments where you're going to get nervous. I'm telling you now, so when it happens you know it's normal. Inspection day. Appraisal. And that last week, when the lender goes quiet and you're certain something's wrong.

Name all three out loud before any of them happen. Because a fear you predicted stops feeling like a warning sign. It starts feeling like your agent knows what they're doing.

*(leans into the lens)*

Quick one for the comments. Be honest with me. Have you ever actually made that call? Not a "congrats, we're under contract" text. A real phone call the day after. Yes or no in the comments. No judgment either way, I just want to know how many of us were ever taught it.

*(sips coffee)*

**Part two. The Tuesday Pulse.**

Same day, every week, every pending deal. Tuesday. Monday's chaos and Friday's too late to fix anything.

Four contacts. Your client, the other agent, the lender, and title or your attorney. Four short touches, ten minutes total. Same question every time: "anything you're waiting on that I can move today?"

Here's why this works, and it's got nothing to do with information. Half the time the answer is "nope, we're fine." That is not a wasted call. That's the call where your client hears your voice on an ordinary Tuesday when nothing's wrong. That's the deposit. You're not withdrawing anything. You're putting your voice in the account so it's already there the week something breaks.

And that deal you're holding? The one that tightens your stomach? Almost time. Two more minutes. Stay with me.

**Part three. The Wobble Window.**

There are three moments where deals actually die. Inspection results. Appraisal. Clear to close.

NAR's June numbers say roughly one in fourteen contracts got delayed by an appraisal problem alone. That's just the appraisal.

The rule for the Wobble Window is one sentence long. You go first.

You call before they call you. The inspection report lands, and you don't forward it. You call, and you walk them through it before they've read page four and decided the house is falling down. The appraisal comes back, you call, good news or bad. The lender goes quiet in week four, you don't wait. You call the lender, then you call your client and tell them you called the lender.

Going first is the entire thing.

When your client calls you with a problem, you're a service provider handling a complaint. When you call your client with the same problem, you're the professional running the deal. Identical information. Completely different relationship.

Day Zero. Tuesday Pulse. Wobble Window. That's the Grip.

And that champagne text? It still goes out. It just stops being the last thing you send.

## SEGMENT 4: THE HARD OBJECTION (~11:00 – ~12:45)

D.J.: Now I know exactly what a bunch of you are thinking, because I've thought it myself.

*(beat)*

"D.J., come on. Most of those deals die over financing, or the inspection, or the buyer just gets scared. That's not a communication problem. I can't phone somebody into qualifying for a loan."

*(sips coffee)*

Fair. Genuinely fair. And you're right, you can't underwrite the loan.

Here's the answer though. Look at what Redfin's agents said was actually driving cancellations this spring. It wasn't only loan denials. They pointed at buyers having other homes to go to, and at buyers getting spooked about the economy and their own job security.

Read that again. That's not underwriting. That's fear, plus options.

You can't control the underwriting. You have complete control over whether a scared person with options hears from you before they decide.

And here's the specific piece. On the Day Zero Call, ask this exact question: "if something in the next month made you want to walk away from this house, what would it be?"

Write down what they say. That sentence is the deal's real risk file, in their words instead of yours. Then every Tuesday, you're listening for that one thing.

You didn't remove the risk. You just stopped getting blindsided by it.

---

## SEGMENT 5: THE MATH/MOTIVATION (~12:45 – ~14:15)

D.J.: Let's do the math on what this actually costs you.

Say you put fourteen deals under contract this year. Good year. Not a superstar year, a solid one. At the national rate, two of them never close.

Not two leads. Not two listing appointments. Two deals where you already did the showings, wrote the offer, won the negotiation, ordered the inspection. The whole job. Zero dollars.

*(beat)*

And that's only the commission. Here's the part that compounds.

Every collapsed deal is also a client who never refers you, never reviews you, and tells the story at dinner as "our deal fell apart." *(beat)* Not "our agent fought for it." You lost the check and the pipeline in the same week.

Now flip it. You don't have to save both. Save one. One deal a year, held together by a phone call you were already completely capable of making.

That's the math nobody puts in a listing presentation. The cheapest deal you'll win all year is the one you already have.

*(sets the coffee down)*

Which brings me back to that deal you've been holding.

---

## CLOSE (~14:15 – ~15:00)

D.J.: That deal you've been holding this whole time. Here's exactly what you do tonight.

Open your texts and send this: "Hey [Name], no news, nothing's wrong. I just wanted you to hear from me. Anything you're wondering about that I haven't answered?"

Send it before you go to bed.

Then two more. Set a repeating Tuesday reminder on every pending file you've got. And tomorrow morning, call the lender on your oldest one.

*(beat)*

You've been telling yourself you're the agent who gets the deal. Agents who keep deals alive treat the signature as the starting gun, not the finish line. You just fired it.

*(raises coffee)*

The contract isn't the closing. I'll see you next time.

END
