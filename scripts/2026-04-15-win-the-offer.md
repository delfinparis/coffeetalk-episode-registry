SEGMENT 2: WHY IT HAPPENS (~3:30 – ~6:30)

D.J.: Before the fix, let's talk about why agents keep ending up in this spot. Because it's not about effort. Agents in multiple offer situations are working hard. They're running comps, they're writing offers, they're doing what they were trained to do.

TIM: So what's actually going wrong?

D.J.: Three things. They all feed each other. First one: price-first thinking. When most agents hear "multiple offers," their brain jumps straight to the number. How high can we go? What's the max the buyer will stretch? Price becomes the whole strategy. It feels like the most controllable thing.

TIM: It's the lever everyone knows how to pull.

D.J.: It's the loudest lever. But it's not the only one. Second thing. Nobody calls.

TIM: Nobody calls the listing agent?

D.J.: Before the offer is submitted. Before they've committed to anything. Most buyers' agents never pick up the phone and call the listing agent. Never ask: what matters to your seller? Is there anything besides price that's come up? What would make this easier?

TIM: That call takes five minutes.

D.J.: Five minutes. And the intel from it is worth more than a $10,000 escalation. But most agents skip it. It feels awkward, or they assume the listing agent won't say anything, or they just don't think to do it.

TIM: What's the third thing?

D.J.: Third: they're writing the offer to the buyer, not the seller. They're thinking about what the buyer can afford, what protects the buyer, what the buyer wants. All valid. But a winning offer is written for the seller. What closes the gap between what they have and what they need to feel safe saying yes.

TIM: Writing to the wrong audience.

D.J.: Writing to the wrong audience. Okay. Here's what I want you to do right now, before we get to the fix. Think of a buyer you're currently working with. Someone in a competitive area, or someone who's already lost an offer this year. Just one. Hold that buyer in your head. Don't say who, just hold them. We're going to do something specific for them before we sign off today.

TIM: I've already got mine.

D.J.: Good. Because by the time we sign off, you'll know exactly what to do for that buyer this week. Let's get into it.

SEGMENT 3: THE SYSTEM/SOLUTION (~6:30 – ~11:00)

D.J.: The Certainty Stack. Four layers. When you build all four into an offer, you're not just competing on price. You're competing on the thing the seller actually cares about. That this deal closes.

TIM: Walk me through it.

D.J.: Layer one. The Pre-Underwrite. Most buyers show up with a pre-approval letter. You know what a pre-approval letter is to a sophisticated listing agent?

TIM: Something tells me it's less impressive than I thought.

D.J.: It's a letter that says "a lender looked at this buyer's income and credit for about 20 minutes and thinks they can probably get a loan." That's it. A pre-underwrite is different. With a pre-underwrite (some lenders call it a credit-approved loan) the underwriting is already done. Conditions are met. The lender just needs the property address to close.

TIM: So it's as close to a cash offer as a financed buyer can get.

D.J.: As close as it gets. When a listing agent sees that letter, they read it differently. That stack of offers on the table suddenly has one that says: this deal will close.

TIM: And step one is you have to talk to your lender before you're ever in a competitive situation. Not during.

D.J.: Before. Not every lender offers it. Good lenders do, and it's free. If your buyer is looking in a hot area and doesn't have one, make that call today. Layer two.

D.J.: The Intel Call. Before your offer goes in, you call the listing agent. Doesn't matter whether you think they'll share anything. You make the call. And you don't ask about the offer. You ask about the seller.

TIM: What's the difference?

D.J.: "What's the seller looking for" gets you nothing. "Is there anything about the timing that would make this easier for your clients" gets you everything. Now you're asking about their life. Their move date, their next purchase, whether they need 30 days post-close occupancy.

TIM: Okay. I want to push back on that. I've made that call. And a lot of the time I get "submit your best offer, we'll review Sunday, good luck." Door closed. What do you actually do when the listing agent shuts you down?

D.J.: Two things. First. You still asked. And that puts you on their radar as an agent who does homework. That matters more than people think when they're sorting through a pile of offers. Second: if they won't talk timeline, ask about the property. "Is there anything about the condition of the home that's come up that we should know about?" Sometimes what the seller is most anxious about is a repair they know exists. If you know that, you know exactly what language to put in your offer.

TIM: You're gathering intel even when they say they won't give you any.

D.J.: Every answer is information. Including "I can't share that." Layer three.

D.J.: The Timeline Mirror. Most underused layer in the whole stack. It costs your buyer nothing. You find out when the seller wants to close. If they need to stay after closing, that's called a rent-back. And then you match it exactly.

TIM: Not "we're flexible." Actually mirror the date.

D.J.: Mirror it. Seller wants 45 days? Your offer says 45 days. Seller needs a 30-day rent-back? Done, no rent charged. It costs the buyer nothing. And to the seller it reads as: these people get it. This is the easy offer.

TIM: That sounds almost too simple to matter.

D.J.: It's that simple. And almost nobody does it because they never made the intel call to find out what the seller actually needed. Layers two and three are the same move. Layer four.

D.J.: The Clean Escalation. Most agents either avoid escalation clauses because they feel like showing your hand. Or they use them wrong. They escalate in $1,000 increments up to a cap the listing agent does the math on in 10 seconds.

TIM: "They'll go to $520,000. Got it."

D.J.: A clean escalation starts at or above asking, escalates in meaningful increments ($5,000 minimum), and has a cap that sits above where you estimate the top offer will land. You're not showing desperation. You're showing confidence. There's a difference.

TIM: (to camera) Real question for the agents watching. Have you ever won a multiple offer situation with an escalation clause? Drop what worked in the comments. Genuinely curious what you've seen.

D.J.: And that buyer you're holding in your head right now. We're almost there. Two more minutes, you'll know exactly what to do for them.

SEGMENT 4: THE HARD OBJECTION (~11:00 – ~12:45)

TIM: I have to say the thing out loud that a lot of agents watching are thinking. You've talked about contingencies a few times. At some point we have to deal with the inspection conversation. Because the second you suggest reducing the inspection contingency to win an offer, half the people watching think about that one client who got burned. I had a buyer waive inspection. Found a foundation issue after close. Forty thousand dollars. So when you tell me to touch the inspection contingency, I push back hard on that.

D.J.: You should. Waiving inspection wholesale is not what I'm recommending. And in most markets, it's not what you need to win. Here are three options that work without playing roulette with your buyer.

Option one: pre-offer walkthrough. Not a full inspection. No report, no contingencies. You call a contractor or inspector you trust, you walk the property for 30 minutes before the offer is written. You're looking for the big stuff. Takes one afternoon and it costs a couple hundred dollars.

TIM: So you've done your homework before you're ever in the negotiation.

D.J.: You've done your homework. Option two: inspection with a repair cap. You keep the contingency. Write in: "buyer accepts property in current condition for repair items under $X." Five thousand, ten thousand, whatever fits the market. The seller reads that as: we're not going to nickel-and-dime you. But we're not walking into a money pit either.

TIM: What does that language actually look like in the offer?

D.J.: Write it as: "Buyer accepts the property in its current condition as it relates to repair items under $[amount]. Buyer reserves the right to negotiate or void for repair items exceeding this threshold." That exact sentence. Copy it into your next offer.

TIM: Option three?

D.J.: Shorten the timeline. Keep the full contingency. Go from 10 days to five. Sellers read a tight inspection period as: these buyers are organized, they won't drag this out. Sometimes that alone tips the decision.

D.J.: Take a buyer who loses three offers over three months before winning one. That's not unusual in a competitive market. Three months of rent at [STAT NEEDED: verified median rent figure] a month. That's [calculated total from verified figure] paid toward nothing while they keep searching.

TIM: And the market's not sitting still while they wait.

D.J.: Not even close. On a [STAT NEEDED: verified appreciation rate] appreciating market, three months of sitting out costs real money in equity they never built. By the time they win, the house they wanted in January costs more in April.

D.J.: At minimum. And that's just money. That's not the phone call where your buyer says "I think I need to try working with someone else." That's not the referral they would have given you if you'd won the first time.

TIM: Every buyer you lose to a failed offer is a sphere of influence that walks out with them.

D.J.: Every single one. The Certainty Stack doesn't just win offers. It protects your business.

CLOSE (~14:15 – ~15:00)

D.J.: Alright. That buyer you've been holding. Here's exactly what you do this week. One. Call your lender today and ask about pre-underwriting. If they offer it, get it done before the next offer. Two. Before that offer goes in, make the intel call. Ask about timing. Ask about the seller's situation. Three. Mirror the seller's timeline in your offer exactly. Don't estimate. Match it.

TIM: Three moves. This week. Not someday.

D.J.: Here's what I want to say to everyone watching. You didn't tune into this show to "try this sometime." That's the old story. Good intentions, same result, offer after offer. The agents who win competitive situations aren't luckier. They run the system. You just learned the system. That's who you are now.

TIM: Go make the call.

D.J.: The highest offer doesn't always win. The clearest offer almost always does. See you next week.
