COLD OPEN (0:00 – ~0:45)

D.J.: (sets coffee down, looks at camera) Fifty-nine.

TIM: Fifty-nine what?

D.J.: The median age of the American homebuyer. Right now. Not the oldest buyer. The median.

TIM: I thought that number was somewhere in the thirties.

D.J.: It used to be. Most agents still market like it is. Which is why so many of you are wondering where the business went. I'm D.J. Paris. That's Tim Littleton. Today we're talking about the buyer who's actually buying. And why almost every agent is aiming their entire business at someone who isn't in the market anymore. Stay with us.

---

SEGMENT 1: THE PROBLEM (~0:45 – ~3:30)

D.J.: Pull up any agent's Instagram right now. Go look. What are you going to see?

TIM: Same stuff on every feed.

D.J.: Rent versus buy math. First-time buyer workshops. How much do you need for a down payment. Posts aimed at thirty-somethings thinking about their first home.

TIM: That's been the playbook for twenty years.

D.J.: That's been the playbook. Now here's the NAR 2025 Profile of Home Buyers and Sellers. Released this year. Covers every closed transaction from July 2024 through June 2025. Consumer-reported. Largest data set of buyer and seller behavior we have.

TIM: What does it say?

D.J.: First-time buyers are 21% of the market.

TIM: 21%.

D.J.: Record low. Lowest since NAR started tracking in 1981. For most of the last forty years that number sat around 40%. Almost half the market used to be first-timers. Now one in five.

TIM: One in five. So four out of five buyers, your content isn't talking to them.

D.J.: Four out of five. And it gets sharper. The median age of the American homebuyer right now is fifty-nine. Not thirty-five. Not forty-two. Fifty-nine.

TIM: I want to actually process that. The typical buyer in this country is nearly sixty years old.

D.J.: Nearly sixty. Boomers are 42% of all buyers. And they're 55% of all sellers. Here's what that looks like. Four out of every ten homes are going to a boomer buyer. More than five out of every ten came from a boomer seller.

TIM: So when I'm sitting there making a TikTok about closing costs for first-time buyers.

D.J.: You're making it for 21% of the market. And there are fifty thousand other agents making the exact same TikTok. You're not losing to other agents. You're losing to the wrong audience.

TIM: That hits.

D.J.: One more. 26% of buyers in the last year paid all cash. Highest share on record. That's more than one in four people writing offers who don't need a lender. Don't care about rate. Don't care about affordability calculators. Writing a check.

TIM: And most of us don't have a single piece of content speaking to that buyer.

D.J.: Almost none of us do.

TIM: If that number hits you, type the age of your last buyer in the comments. Just the number. I'm betting most of you are under forty.

D.J.: Stick around. We're about to show you who you should actually be talking to and how.

---

SEGMENT 2: WHY IT HAPPENS (~3:30 – ~6:30)

D.J.: Before we get to the fix, let's talk about how we got here. Because it's not a marketing failure. It's a training failure.

TIM: How so?

D.J.: Every brokerage training program you and I sat through came out of the 2010s. Maybe earlier. Back when the biggest buyer pool in America was the millennial first-time buyer. Mortgage rates were three-and-a-half percent. Inventory was cheap. Young people were coming into the market in waves. So the whole apparatus around agent marketing, lead generation, social media, all of it, got built for that buyer.

TIM: And then the buyer moved.

D.J.: The buyer moved. Affordability collapsed. Mortgage rates doubled. Student loan debt. Rents up. First-time buyers couldn't buy anymore. Meanwhile, boomers have equity. A paid-off house, or close to paid off. Low basis, high current value. They're the only buyers right now who can actually make the math work.

TIM: So we trained a whole generation of agents for a market that no longer exists.

D.J.: Trained for a market that no longer exists. That's reason one. Reason two. Agents don't know where boomer buyers come from. If I ask an agent "how do you find your buyers," they say Zillow leads, Instagram reach-outs, open houses, Facebook ads. None of those reach a fifty-nine-year-old with a paid-off house.

TIM: Where does that buyer come from?

D.J.: Their CPA. Their estate attorney. Their financial advisor. Their church. Their golf league. Their adult kids. A decision that big, they don't fill out a form on Zillow. They ask someone they trust.

TIM: And most agents don't have a single relationship in any of those categories.

D.J.: Most don't. Reason three is the hardest one to hear. Agents don't think they're allowed to pivot. There's a voice in your head that says "I'm a family agent" or "I'm a first-time buyer agent" and you think that's your identity. It's not. It's a marketing choice you made in 2019 when the market was different.

TIM: And the market changed without asking us.

D.J.: It always does. Okay. Before we get to the fix, here's what I want you to do right now. Think of one person in your world who's fifty-five or older. Someone who owns their home. Maybe they paid it off. Maybe they have a tiny mortgage. Maybe they're an empty nester in a house that's too big for them. Just one person. Don't say who. Just hold that person in your head. We're going to do something with them before we sign off today.

TIM: I've got mine.

D.J.: Good. Hold on to them. Let's get into the fix.

---

SEGMENT 3: THE SYSTEM/SOLUTION (~6:30 – ~11:00)

D.J.: The Equity Shift. Four moves. When you run all four, you stop being the agent everyone ignores and you start being the agent the market is actually looking for. Layer one.

D.J.: The Content Pivot. Go look at your last ten posts. Count how many speak to a buyer under forty. Count how many speak to a homeowner over fifty-five who's sitting on equity and deciding what to do with it.

TIM: For most of us, the score is ten to zero.

D.J.: Ten to zero. Here's the pivot. Your content for the next thirty days: downsizing tradeoffs. What your 1995 home is actually worth today. Whether to help your adult child buy before passing the house to them. Rising property tax implications for long-term owners. Reverse mortgage myths. Equity repositioning.

TIM: None of that is sexy.

D.J.: None of that is sexy to a twenty-five-year-old marketing director. All of it is urgent for a sixty-two-year-old who's been sitting in that house for twenty years. You're not making content for the algorithm. You're making content for a real person with a real decision. Layer two.

D.J.: The Referral Pivot. Four relationships. That's all you need. One CPA. One estate planning attorney. One financial advisor. One elder-care or downsizing consultant. If you have zero of those right now, build one a month for four months.

TIM: What's the ask when you sit down with them?

D.J.: "I want to be the real estate person you send your clients to when they're deciding what to do with a paid-off house." That's the sentence. Not "send me leads." Not "let's cross-refer." They'll ask what makes you different from the other fifty agents who called. You tell them: "I specialize in equity-rich sellers and all-cash buyers. Most agents don't."

TIM: Pause for a second. For the agents watching. If you have one CPA or one estate attorney who actually sends you business, drop their first name in the comments. Everyone else, that's your gap. And we all have gaps.

D.J.: That's the gap. Layer three.

D.J.: The Listing Presentation Pivot. Most listing presentations pitch "we'll market to every buyer." Most common line in the industry. It's also garbage. You can't market to every buyer. Nobody can. So when you walk into a listing appointment and the seller asks "who's going to buy my house," most agents say something vague about internet marketing and open houses.

TIM: What do you say instead?

D.J.: You name the buyer. "Your buyer is fifty-nine. They've sold a bigger house. They're looking for one level, low maintenance, good neighbors. Here's where they're coming from. Here's how we reach them." That's a listing presentation. That's an agent who did homework.

TIM: And the seller hears the difference.

D.J.: They hear it immediately. Layer four.

D.J.: The Sphere Pivot. Open your phone right now. Open your contacts. Scroll. How many people in there are over fifty-five? How many own their home? How many have you had a real conversation with in the last six months?

TIM: Brutal question.

D.J.: For most agents, it's close to zero. It's also your easiest pipeline. That person you're holding in your head right now. We're almost there. Stay with us. One more segment and you'll know exactly what to do with them.

---

SEGMENT 4: THE HARD OBJECTION (~11:00 – ~12:45)

TIM: I need to voice the pushback. Because I can hear it from the agents watching. A lot of people are thinking this right now. "D.J., I already work with older clients. Half my sphere is over fifty-five. This isn't new to me." So what do you say to them?

D.J.: Working with them isn't the same as marketing to them. Those are two different things. If I ask you "how many of your last ten closings were with clients over fifty-five," and you say seven, great. Now ask the second question. How many of those seven came from your marketing? How many came from someone you already knew calling you when they were ready?

TIM: Almost always the second one.

D.J.: Almost always. So here's the problem. You're servicing boomer clients. You're not attracting them. Which means when that sphere dries up, when people move, when friends of friends stop remembering your name, you don't have a system to replace them. You're playing defense. The Equity Shift is offense.

TIM: So even the agent who thinks this doesn't apply probably needs layer two and layer three the most.

D.J.: Probably needs them the most. They've got the expertise. They just don't have a marketing system behind it.

---

SEGMENT 5: THE MATH/MOTIVATION (~12:45 – ~14:15)

D.J.: Let's do the math on what staying with the old playbook costs you. Say you run the first-time buyer playbook. You're aimed at 21% of the market. And within that 21%, you're competing with every agent in your city who got the same training you did.

TIM: Crowded pool.

D.J.: Crowded pool, small pond. Now look at the other side. 42% of the market is boomer buyers. 55% of sellers are boomers. More than one in four buyers is paying cash. And most agents have zero content, zero referral relationships, and zero listing presentation strategy for any of them.

TIM: Open lane.

D.J.: Open lane. Average sale price on a boomer move is higher. Transaction velocity is faster because there's more cash and less financing friction. Commission check is bigger. Less competition for the business.

TIM: So you're working less, for more, with more certainty.

D.J.: Working less, for more, with more certainty. And every month you spend fighting in the first-time buyer pool is a month the agent down the street who made the pivot is building a pipeline you'll never catch.

TIM: Last line.

D.J.: Equity doesn't retire. Agents who chase it don't either.

---

CLOSE (~14:15 – ~15:00)

D.J.: Alright. That person you've been holding. Over fifty-five. Owns their home. Here's exactly what you do today. Open your phone. Text them: "Hey [Name]. I was looking at some data on what homeowners your age with equity are actually doing right now. Not what most people think. Buy you coffee next week?"

TIM: One text. Thirty seconds.

D.J.: You're not a first-time buyer agent. That's the old story from a market that doesn't exist. You just sent a text to a sixty-two-year-old with equity. That's what agents who run the current market do. Not someday. Now.

TIM: Go send it.

D.J.: Your buyer is fifty-nine. Time to catch up. See you next week.

---
