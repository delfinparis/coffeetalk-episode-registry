Coffee Talk with Tim & D.J.
Episode: "The Day-One Number"
Runtime: 15 minutes
Format: Live stream to all social channels
Tone: Two buddies having coffee, dropping truth bombs

COLD OPEN (0:00 – ~0:45)

D.J.: *(setting down coffee)* Seventeen and a half percent.

TIM: Seventeen and a half percent of what?

D.J.: Of homes on the market right now had to cut their price. That's the number from May. And here's the part nobody's talking about. That number went down from last year.

TIM: Wait, prices are dropping everywhere and fewer sellers are cutting?

D.J.: Fewer. Even with list prices falling at the steepest pace since 2017. I'm D.J. Paris, that's Tim Littleton, and today we're going to show you why the sellers who never cut their price are the ones who priced lowest on day one. If you've got a listing appointment this week, stay right here. This changes how you walk in the door.

SEGMENT 1: THE PROBLEM (~0:45 – ~3:30)

TIM: Okay, walk me through this, because it sounds backwards. Prices are sliding, and you're telling me fewer homes are getting price cuts?

D.J.: That's exactly it. Realtor.com put out their May report this month. Seventeen and a half percent of active listings had a price reduction. A year ago it was higher, by about a point and a half. So in a softer market, fewer sellers are slashing.

TIM: *(leaning back)* That genuinely surprises me. I'd have bet the other way.

D.J.: Everybody would. Because here's the story agents tell themselves. "The market's dropping, so of course sellers have to chase it down." But the data says the opposite is happening. The sellers who came in with a realistic number on day one, they're not chasing anything. They're already sold.

TIM: So the price cuts are coming from a specific group.

D.J.: One group. The sellers who listed high, sat there, and then panicked. And here's the thing about a price cut. It's not a quiet little adjustment. Every buyer's agent sees it. Every portal flags it. A price cut is a public confession that you got it wrong the first time.

TIM: And buyers smell blood.

D.J.: They circle. They wait for the next one. Meanwhile the home that priced right on day one had three showings the first weekend and went under contract before the overpriced one ever dropped a dollar. That's the gap we're fixing today. Your listing doesn't get punished for a low price. It gets punished for a wrong price.

TIM: Say that one more time.

D.J.: Your seller never loses money on the right price. They lose money on the wrong one. The market doesn't care what your seller needs to net. It cares what the house is worth this week.

SEGMENT 2: WHY IT HAPPENS (~3:30 – ~6:30)

TIM: So why do agents keep letting sellers list high? Everybody knows overpricing is a trap.

D.J.: Two reasons, and neither one is stupidity. The first is the listing appointment itself. You're sitting across from a seller who wants a big number. If you give them a number they love, you win the listing. The agent who tells the truth sometimes loses it to the agent who tells the fantasy.

TIM: So we're rewarded for lying.

D.J.: In the short term, yes. You win the listing, then you spend six weeks slowly walking that seller back down to the number you knew on day one. Two months of stress to land where honesty would've put you in a week.

TIM: And the second reason?

D.J.: The seller's anchor. They saw their neighbor's house list for a number eighteen months ago, in a different market. That price is burned into their brain. They're not pricing today's home. They're pricing a memory.

TIM: That one's real. I hear it every week. "But the house down the street got way more than that."

D.J.: And it did. In a market that's gone. List prices just posted their steepest annual drop since 2017. Seven straight months down. The neighbor's number isn't a comp. It's a ghost.

D.J.: Now before we get into the fix, I want you to do one thing. Think of one listing. A real one. Either a listing you have right now that's sitting, or a listing appointment you've got coming up this week. Picture the address. Don't say it out loud. Just hold that one listing in your head.

TIM: I've got mine. There's one sitting right now.

D.J.: Perfect. Hold onto that exact listing. We're going to do something specific with it before we sign off. Because the difference between that listing selling and that listing rotting comes down to one number, and we're about to name it.

SEGMENT 3: THE SYSTEM/SOLUTION (~6:30 – ~11:00)

D.J.: Here's the framework. I call it the Day-One Number. Three parts. The whole point is to set the right price before the home ever hits the market, so your seller never has to make that public confession we talked about.

TIM: The price cut.

D.J.: The price cut. Part one. The Seven-Day Window. You tell your seller this on the listing appointment, out loud, in plain language. The first week your home is live is the most attention it will ever get in its entire life. The most. Every buyer who's been waiting, every agent with a matching client, they all see it in those first seven days. If you price for week one, you sell in week one. If you price for your dream, you spend week one alone.

TIM: So the first week isn't a soft launch. It's the whole game.

D.J.: It's the Super Bowl, and most sellers treat it like a preseason scrimmage. They figure they'll test a high number and adjust later. But you can't get week one back. When the price cut finally comes, the fresh buyers are gone. You're showing to leftovers.

TIM: Okay. Part two.

D.J.: Part two. The Honest Bracket. Don't give your seller one number. Give them a range, and be dead honest about what each end means. Top of the range, here's how long you'll likely sit. Bottom of the range, here's how fast you'll likely sell. You're not telling them what to pick. You're showing them the trade. Speed costs a little. Greed costs a lot.

TIM: I like that you're not just telling them to price low.

D.J.: Because that's not the message. I'm not telling anyone to give the house away. I'm telling them to pick a number on purpose, with their eyes open, instead of throwing a high number at the wall and hoping. The Honest Bracket makes the seller the decision-maker. They own the choice.

TIM: That listing I'm holding? I think I priced the wrong end of the bracket.

D.J.: Hold that thought. That listing you're picturing, almost time. Stay with us, because part three is where it pays off. Part three. The Day-One Number itself. This is the single price you'd both bet on. Not the aspirational number, not the floor. The number where you look your seller in the eye and say, at this price, I believe we sell in the first seven days. You write it down. You date it. And if you ever talk price again, you start from that number, not from the fantasy.

TIM: Hold on, I want to push back on this.

D.J.: Go.

TIM: This sounds clean in a coffee shop. But in the real world, the seller across from me has a number they need to net. They've got a down payment on the next house riding on it. I can talk about brackets all day, they're going to say, "Tim, I can't take less than X." What do I do with that?

D.J.: That's the right pushback, and it's the realest one there is. Here's the honest answer. You don't argue with their need. Their need is real. But the market doesn't pay sellers based on need. It pays based on worth. So you separate the two out loud. You say, "I hear what you need to net. Let's look at what the house will actually bring this week, and then let's see if there's a gap." Sometimes there's no gap and you're fine. Sometimes there's a gap, and now you're solving a real problem together instead of pretending the high price makes the gap disappear. Hiding the gap behind a fantasy price doesn't close it. It just makes the seller pay for it in time and in cuts.

TIM: So you name the gap instead of burying it.

D.J.: You name it on day one, in private, with your seller, instead of letting the market name it in public, six weeks later, in front of everyone. People watching live, drop the one objection you hear most on listing appointments. I want to see if it's the net number or something else.

SEGMENT 4: THE HARD OBJECTION (~11:00 – ~12:45)

TIM: Here's the one I know is coming. Somebody watching is thinking, "D.J., I've tried the honest-price conversation. I showed them the comps, I laid it all out, and they still listed high with the next agent who flattered them. So why bother?"

D.J.: I believe that, because it happens. Here's the fix, and it's one specific move. Stop presenting comps as your opinion. Present them as the buyer's behavior. Don't say, "I think it's worth this." Say, "Here are the four homes a buyer will compare yours to this weekend, and here's what each one is asking." You're not the one delivering the bad news anymore. The competition is.

TIM: So you take yourself out of the fight.

D.J.: Completely. The seller can argue with your opinion all day. They can't argue with four live listings the buyer is going to tour right after they leave yours. That's not D.J.'s number. That's the market's number, sitting on the same street. Your action step. Before your next listing appointment, pull the four closest active competitors and put them on one page. Not solds. Actives. What the buyer is choosing between right now.

TIM: Actives, not solds. Because the buyer isn't shopping the past.

D.J.: The buyer's shopping today. Show them today.

SEGMENT 5: THE MATH/MOTIVATION (~12:45 – ~14:15)

D.J.: Let me put real numbers on this. Newly pending sales just rose for the sixth straight month. Buyers are out there, signing, right now. So the cost of overpricing isn't theoretical. While your overpriced listing sits, the buyers who would've signed are signing on other homes.

TIM: They don't wait for you to come to your senses.

D.J.: They never do. And here's the loss. Active inventory's up a little over two percent from last year, so your seller has more competition than they did, not less. Every week that listing sits stale, it gets harder, not easier. The first week was the asset. Overpricing spends it.

TIM: So the high price doesn't protect their equity. It burns it.

D.J.: It burns it in slow motion. A right-priced home gets the showings, gets the offers, sometimes gets multiple, and the right price ends up selling for more than the wrong price ever would. The seller who chased the fantasy nets less than the seller who named the Day-One Number. Every single time the market lets us measure it.

CLOSE (~14:15 – ~15:00)

D.J.: Alright. Seventeen and a half percent of listings cut their price, and the ones who didn't are the ones who got it right on day one. That listing you've been holding in your head. Here's exactly what you do right now. Open your photos or your notes app and write down two things. One, the Day-One Number you'd actually bet sells it in the first week. Two, the four active listings a buyer would tour instead. That's the page you walk in with.

TIM: One number. Four competitors. One page.

D.J.: Here's the shift. You've been telling yourself you're the agent who fights to get sellers the most money. That's the old story, and it's why your listings sit. The agents whose listings sell are the ones who price for the buyer who exists, not the buyer they wish were coming. You just wrote down a number you'd bet on. That's not pricing low. That's pricing to sell, which is the only job your seller hired you for.

TIM: Go write the number.

D.J.: The market never punishes the right price. It only collects on the wrong one. We'll see you next week.

END
