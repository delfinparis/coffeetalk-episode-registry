COLD OPEN (0:00 – ~0:45)

D.J.: (sets coffee down, looks at camera) Eighty-one percent.

TIM: Eighty-one percent of what?

D.J.: Of sellers last year. They contacted exactly one agent before signing.

TIM: One? They didn't even interview anybody else?

D.J.: Just one. NAR Profile of Home Buyers and Sellers. Eight in ten sellers. They'd already made up their mind before any agent walked through the door.

TIM: (beat) So all those listing presentations we've been losing sleep over.

D.J.: I'm D.J. Paris. That's Tim Littleton. Today we're showing you why most agents win the listing six months before the seller picks up the phone. And what to do about it starting today. Stay with us.

---

SEGMENT 1: THE PROBLEM (~0:45 – ~3:30)

D.J.: Let me give you the full number. NAR 2024 Profile of Home Buyers and Sellers. The largest annual survey of who actually sold a home and how they picked their agent. 81% of those sellers contacted only one agent.

TIM: That's wild. So when an agent says "I lost the listing," they didn't really lose it. They were never in the running.

D.J.: They weren't in the running. They got called for a courtesy. Or they didn't get called at all.

TIM: (shaking head) I've been on so many of those calls. Drive across town, set up the comps, do the whole presentation. And the seller already had Linda from down the block in mind the whole time.

D.J.: That's exactly it. Linda was already in. You were the comparison shop. Or the seller's spouse made them get a "second opinion" out of guilt.

TIM: (laughing) The guilt presentation. Brutal. If you've sat through one of those, tell us about it in the comments. Three sentences or less. Tim and I want to hear it.

D.J.: Here's the part that should sting a little. About four in ten sellers picked their agent through a referral from a friend, family member, or neighbor. NAR same source. Add the past clients who came back on their own and the math gets ugly fast.

TIM: Ugly how?

D.J.: Ugly like, the average agent isn't losing listing presentations. They're losing everything that happens before the listing presentation. Months of ground game they didn't run.

TIM: So the work isn't the meeting. The work is everything that gets you into the meeting in the first place.

D.J.: And if you're not the only call, you've already lost. Because most sellers only make one.

TIM: (leaning back) Okay. So if 81% of sellers aren't interviewing me, the whole script-coaching empire I've been signing up for, those "21 ways to nail a listing presentation" courses.

D.J.: It's solving for the wrong eighty percent of the problem.

TIM: That's the line right there.

---

SEGMENT 2: WHY IT HAPPENS (~3:30 – ~6:30)

D.J.: So why do most sellers only call one agent?

TIM: I want to hear this.

D.J.: Three reasons. First, real estate is a low-frequency, high-stakes decision. People aren't shopping. They're getting it over with.

TIM: Like picking a doctor. You don't interview five oncologists.

D.J.: Right. You take the one your sister-in-law swears by and you go.

TIM: That tracks.

D.J.: Second reason. Sellers are emotionally exhausted before they even start. Decluttering, repairs, the kid going to college, divorce, parents moving in. By the time the For Sale sign goes up, they've already spent their decision-making fuel.

TIM: So they don't want options. They want one trusted name. They want to move on.

D.J.: Exactly. Choice fatigue. And the third reason. This one hurts. Most agents are forgettable. Their last text to a past client was "Happy holidays" in a group blast. Their feed is twelve listings in a row. Nothing memorable. Nothing personal.

TIM: So when the moment comes, the seller doesn't think of you. They think of the agent who's been actually present.

D.J.: And the agent who's actually present isn't always the best agent. It's the one who stayed warm.

TIM: Stayed warm. I like that.

D.J.: Okay, before we get into the system, I want everybody watching to do one thing. Think of one person in your life right now. Someone you genuinely think will sell their home in the next twelve months. Not a maybe. Someone you have real reason to believe will list. A friend. A neighbor. A sibling. A coworker. A past client who's outgrown their place.

TIM: I've already got someone.

D.J.: Hold their name. Don't text them yet. Don't say it out loud. Just hold it. We're going to do something specific with that name before we sign off, and you're going to send it before you do anything else today.

TIM: I see somebody in the comments saying "I've got mine." Good. Hold it. We're coming back to it.

---

SEGMENT 3: THE SYSTEM/SOLUTION (~6:30 – ~11:00)

D.J.: Okay. The system. We call it The Only-Call List. The whole goal is simple. When that person you're holding in your head decides to sell, yours is the only number they call. Three parts.

TIM: Hit me.

D.J.: Part one. The Touch System. This is your past clients. The people who've already trusted you with the biggest financial decision of their life. NAR data says about one in five buyers uses the same agent again. The rest are referral candidates. But only if you stay top of mind.

TIM: One in five. So most past clients won't come back on their own.

D.J.: Most past clients won't come back on their own. Not because they don't like you. Because they forgot you exist. The mistake most agents make is they touch their database four times a year, all blast, all generic. Birthday text. Holiday card. Anniversary email. Market update.

TIM: That's me. I do all four.

D.J.: That's everybody. And that's why nobody remembers it. The Touch System is twelve specific, personal touches per past client per year. One a month. Not a blast. Not a newsletter. A specific text or note that proves you remember who they are. "Saw your kid graduated, congratulations." "Watched the Bears game and thought of you." "Heard your block had a sale at six-twenty. Want to know what it traded for?"

TIM: Hold on. Twelve a year, per client. If I have a hundred past clients.

D.J.: That's twelve hundred touches a year.

TIM: That's a lot of texts.

D.J.: It is. Stick with us. Part three solves it.

D.J.: Part two. The Asker's Edge. NAR says about four in ten sellers come through referrals. But most agents never actually ask for one. They hint. They wait. They hope. The Asker's Edge is one specific ask, three times a year, to your top thirty relationships. Not "let me know if anybody's selling." A real ask. "I'm trying to add three new clients this quarter. Who's the next person you know who's planning to move?"

TIM: That's a different question.

D.J.: It's a completely different question. One puts the burden on them to remember you. The other puts a name in their head right now.

D.J.: And that name you're all holding, by the way, almost time. Two minutes. Stay with us.

D.J.: Part three. The Visibility Loop. This is the content piece. One short video a week. Not market updates. Not "just listed." A real human moment with a real real-estate insight. Forty seconds. Post it on every platform you have.

TIM: A video a week. Most agents I know post for two weeks and then disappear.

D.J.: That's the failure point. The reason most agents fail at this isn't talent. It's frequency. They post for two weeks, get six likes, and quit. The Visibility Loop is the one that compounds. Month four is when your name starts coming up in conversations you weren't in.

TIM: (leaning in) Okay. I want to push back. You're making this sound like a content grind. Most agents I know are already running ragged. You're telling them, what, twelve hundred personal touches a year?

D.J.: Plus the asks. Plus the videos.

TIM: That's a full-time job on top of selling houses.

D.J.: That's the right pushback. Honest answer. It's not a full-time job. It's about thirty minutes a day, run consistently. The Touch System runs through your CRM with a daily list of four people to text personally. Four. Not forty. Four. The Asker's Edge runs three calendar reminders a year. The Visibility Loop is one short video. Film it in your car between showings.

TIM: Thirty minutes.

D.J.: Thirty minutes a day. Or twelve listings you should have had this year. Pick one.

TIM: That's the math.

D.J.: For everyone watching live, if you've already started something like this, drop a one-word answer in the comments. What's working. We want to give the rest of the room something real.

---

SEGMENT 4: THE HARD OBJECTION (~11:00 – ~12:45)

TIM: Okay. The objection I know we're going to get. "I already do this. I send a monthly newsletter. I post on Instagram. I text my past clients on their birthday. I've been doing this for years and it isn't working."

D.J.: I get that one every single week. And here's the honest answer. What you're describing is broadcasting. The Only-Call List is direct contact. Two completely different things.

TIM: Walk me through the difference.

D.J.: A newsletter going out to four hundred people is one touch. Sent four hundred times. It feels like work because it took you all morning. To the recipient, it's a piece of mail. They didn't even open it.

TIM: Okay.

D.J.: The Touch System is one text. You write it for one person. You send it the moment you have a real reason to.

TIM: So if I send the same "thinking of you" text to a hundred clients today, that's still broadcasting.

D.J.: That's broadcasting in disguise. Real contact has a detail. A name. A reference. A reason. If you couldn't have sent that exact message to anybody else, that's a real touch.

TIM: Got it. Specific or it doesn't count.

D.J.: And here's the tool. Open your CRM right now. Filter your past clients by last contact date. The top of that list is your week. Four people a day, hand-written, specific. Not "happy holidays." Real contact.

---

SEGMENT 5: THE MATH/MOTIVATION (~12:45 – ~14:15)

D.J.: Let's put numbers on this. Most agents have somewhere between fifty and two hundred past clients in their database.

TIM: Take the middle.

D.J.: A hundred. Now imagine even a quarter of them buy or sell something over the next five years.

TIM: Twenty-five transactions sitting in your phone right now.

D.J.: At an average sale price of, let's call it four hundred thousand. Two and a half percent commission. That's ten thousand a transaction.

TIM: Twenty-five at ten thousand. A quarter of a million dollars.

D.J.: A quarter of a million in commissions. Sitting in your phone. That you're either going to earn or hand to somebody else.

TIM: That hits.

D.J.: Because most of those clients are only going to make one call. The agent who stayed warm. If that agent isn't you, you're not losing the listing. You never had it.

TIM: You never had it.

D.J.: Every quarter you wait, that money walks out the door and into somebody else's deal.

---

CLOSE (~14:15 – ~15:00)

D.J.: Alright. Three things. Right now.

D.J.: One. The name you've been holding. Open your texts. "Hey [Name], saw something this week that made me think of you. [Specific detail. Their kid's school. Their team. A comp on their block.] Hope you're doing well." Send it before you do anything else after this show.

D.J.: Two. Open your CRM. Sort past clients by last contact. Pick four for tomorrow.

D.J.: Three. Film one short video this week. Forty seconds. In your car between showings.

TIM: That's the whole show.

D.J.: You're not "trying to build a referral business." That's the old story. You just sent the text. That's what agents who are the only call actually do. Today.

TIM: Go send it.

D.J.: Be the only call. We'll see you next week.

---

END
