Coffee Talk with Tim & D.J.
Episode: "The 2.42% Surprise"
Runtime: 15 minutes
Format: Live stream to all social channels
Tone: Two buddies having coffee, dropping truth bombs

COLD OPEN (0:00 – ~0:45)

D.J.: *(setting down coffee)* They went up.

TIM: What went up?

D.J.: Buyer-agent commissions. The whole settlement was supposed to push them down. The headlines, the lawsuit, the four hundred eighteen million dollars. All of it pointed one direction. And then the number went the other way.

TIM: Wait, up? After all that?

D.J.: Up. I'm D.J. Paris, that's Tim Littleton, and today we're going to show you why the thing that was supposed to gut your commission quietly protected it, and exactly which agents got protected. If you've ever worried the settlement was coming for your paycheck, stay right here. This one changes how you walk into your next buyer consult.

SEGMENT 1: THE PROBLEM (~0:45 – ~3:30)

TIM: Okay, give me the number, because I've been bracing for bad news on this for two years.

D.J.: Here it is. The average buyer's-agent commission in this country was two point four two percent in the third quarter of last year. A year earlier, it was two point three six. That's Redfin's December report. So commissions ticked up.

TIM: *(leaning back)* Up. Genuinely, I thought that whole thing was designed to crush this number.

D.J.: Everyone did. That's the gap we're sitting in today. The Sitzer settlement was supposed to be the end of the buyer-agent commission as we knew it. NAR agreed to pay four hundred eighteen million, stop posting buyer pay on the MLS, and make every agent get a signed buyer agreement before a showing. The rules took effect in August of twenty twenty-four.

TIM: And the prediction was a race to the bottom.

D.J.: Every panel, every podcast, said two and a half percent was about to become one. Instead, the number a year later was higher than the day the rules dropped. The floor everybody feared never showed up.

TIM: So what actually happened?

D.J.: A slower market happened. Fewer buyers were out there, so sellers kept offering to pay the buyer's agent to attract the ones who were. And the agents who used those new written agreements, they held their rate instead of folding. The thing built to squeeze you ended up sorting you.

TIM: Sorting how?

D.J.: It separated the agents who can explain what they're worth from the ones who just hoped nobody would ask. Here's the line I want you to sit with. The settlement didn't kill the buyer's agent. It killed the buyer's agent who couldn't answer one question. What exactly do you do for me?

TIM: Say that again.

D.J.: The settlement didn't lower your commission. It put a test in front of it. And plenty of agents are still walking into that test with no answer.

SEGMENT 2: WHY IT HAPPENS (~3:30 – ~6:30)

TIM: So why did the doom prediction miss so badly? Smart people called this wrong.

D.J.: Two reasons, and neither one is luck. The first is the market itself. When buyers are scarce, a seller wants every advantage to pull them in. Offering to cover the buyer's agent is one of the cheapest, most effective magnets a seller has. So sellers kept doing it, quietly, even after the MLS stopped advertising it.

TIM: So the commission didn't disappear. It just went off the billboard.

D.J.: Exactly the right way to put it. It moved from a public posting to a private conversation. And here's the second reason, this is the one that matters for you. The written buyer agreement changed who has the talk and when. Before the settlement, most agents never named their fee out loud. They let the MLS do it. Now you sit down, before a single showing, and you say the number to a buyer's face.

TIM: Which is terrifying for half the agents watching.

D.J.: It is. And that fear is the whole story. The agents who learned to have that conversation held two point four two percent. The agents who flinched, who apologized for their fee, who discounted before the buyer even pushed, those are the ones getting squeezed. The number didn't fall for the industry. It fell for the people who can't say it without wincing.

TIM: Watching live, be honest with us. When a buyer asks how you get paid, do you say your number clean, or do you start apologizing? Drop a one or a two. One if you say it clean, two if you hear yourself softening it.

D.J.: That's the real split right there. Now before we get to the fix, I want you to do one thing. Think of your next buyer. Not a past one. The next buyer consult on your calendar, or the next call you know is coming this week. Picture that person. Don't say the name out loud. Just hold that one buyer in your head.

TIM: Got mine. There's a call Thursday.

D.J.: Perfect. Hold onto that exact buyer. We're going to do something specific with them before we sign off, because the difference between you holding your rate and you giving it away comes down to one conversation, and we're about to script it.

SEGMENT 3: THE SYSTEM/SOLUTION (~6:30 – ~11:00)

D.J.: Here's the framework. I call it Worth Out Loud. Three parts. The whole point is to make your value something you say plainly, before the buyer ever asks, so the agreement signs itself instead of becoming a fight.

TIM: Worth out loud. Because the problem is it's been silent.

D.J.: It's been silent for decades. The MLS said the number for you, so you never learned to. Part one. The Name It First move. You bring up how you get paid before the buyer does. The moment you let the buyer raise it first, you're on defense. You're answering a challenge. When you raise it first, you're just stating how this works. Same number, completely different power. You say, here's what I do, here's what it costs, here's how it gets covered. Calm, early, yours.

TIM: So you're not waiting to get cornered.

D.J.: You never wait. The agent who waits ends up negotiating against themselves. Part two. The Proof Stack. This is where most agents fall apart, because they've never written down what they actually do. So you build a short list, out loud, of the specific work a buyer never sees. You find homes before they hit the portals. You read the inspection like a contractor would. You negotiate repairs the buyer would've missed. You manage the lender, the title company, the deadlines. Each one is a thing that costs the buyer real money if it goes wrong. You're not selling a percentage. You're selling the catch, the save, the deal that didn't fall apart.

TIM: Hold on, let me jump in. Isn't there a risk you just sound like you're bragging?

D.J.: Good catch, and the fix is in how you say it. You don't say I'm great. You say here's what goes wrong without this, and here's how I catch it. It's not about you. It's about the cost of going in alone. Part three. The Choice Frame. You hand the buyer the decision instead of defending your fee. You say, you can absolutely buy a house without me. People do it. Here's what that looks like, and here's what working together looks like, and you pick. The second you make it their choice, you stop being a salesperson they resist. You become the option they'd be crazy to skip.

TIM: That buyer I'm holding from earlier? I've been doing the opposite. I wait, then I justify.

D.J.: Hold that thought, because that's the exact habit we're breaking. That buyer you're picturing, almost time. Stay with me, the payoff's coming in a few minutes.

TIM: Before you keep going, I want to push back on this.

D.J.: Go ahead.

TIM: Here's my pushback, and it's specific. Worth Out Loud sounds great when the buyer has options and money. But I work a price point where buyers are stretched. They've got barely enough for the down payment. I name two and a half percent out loud to that buyer, and I don't look confident. I look greedy. How does this survive a tight budget?

D.J.: That's the realest objection on this topic, and here's the honest answer. You don't change the value talk. You change who pays. With a stretched buyer, you say the number, then immediately, in the same breath, you say, and in most cases we ask the seller to cover this. The buyer hears your worth and your plan to protect their cash at the same moment.

TIM: So you're not hiding the fee. You're showing the path around their wallet.

D.J.: You're naming your worth and solving their problem in one move. Redfin's own data backs the play. Even after the settlement, sellers kept covering buyer agents, because in a slow market they need the buyer more than they need that money. Your job is to ask, out loud, on purpose, every time. People watching, if you've ever gotten the seller to cover your commission in this post-settlement market, tell us the price point you did it at. Somebody watching needs to hear it's possible.

SEGMENT 4: THE HARD OBJECTION (~11:00 – ~12:45)

TIM: Here's the one I know is coming. Somebody watching is thinking, D.J., I tried having the fee conversation up front. I sat a buyer down, I named my number, and they walked. Said they'd find someone cheaper. So why would I ever go first again?

D.J.: I believe that happened, because it does. Here's the fix, and it's one specific move. The buyer didn't walk because you named a fee. They walked because you named a fee before you named your worth. Order is everything. You led with the price tag on a thing they didn't value yet.

TIM: So you flip the sequence.

D.J.: You flip it completely. Proof first, price second. Before the number ever comes up, you walk them through what goes wrong without you. The inspection nobody read. The repair nobody caught. The bidding war nobody coached them through. Now the buyer's a little scared of going alone. Then you name the number, and it sounds like insurance, not a tax. Your action step. Write your Proof Stack tonight. Five specific things you do that a buyer would never see, each one tied to money it saves them. Five. On paper, before your next consult.

TIM: Five saves, on paper, before you ever say the number.

D.J.: Worth out loud only works if you know your worth before you open your mouth.

SEGMENT 5: THE MATH/MOTIVATION (~12:45 – ~14:15)

D.J.: Let me put real money on this. The median REALTOR earned fifty-eight thousand one hundred dollars last year. That's the NAR Member Profile. Now picture an agent who panics and shaves half a point off every buyer deal, just to feel safe, just to not have the conversation.

TIM: Half a point doesn't sound like much.

D.J.: It never does, until you stack it. On the homes under five hundred thousand, the average buyer-side rate was two point five two percent last year, and it's holding. The agent who discounts to two isn't keeping more clients. The data says clients didn't demand it. That agent just handed back a half point of every closing for a fear that wasn't real.

TIM: So the discount didn't buy them anything.

D.J.: It bought them less income and the same number of deals. That's the loss. Not a dramatic blowup. A slow leak, every closing, all year, because they'd rather discount than speak. Meanwhile the agent who says their number clean keeps the full rate the market already proved buyers will pay.

CLOSE (~14:15 – ~15:00)

D.J.: Alright. Commissions went up after the settlement, and the agents who held them are the ones who learned to say their worth out loud. That next buyer you've been holding in your head. Here's exactly what you do right now. Open your notes and type the first line you'll lead with on that consult. Something like, before we look at homes, let me tell you what I actually do for you and how it gets paid. Write it now, so it's ready Thursday.

TIM: One opening line. Said first, not last.

D.J.: Here's the shift. You've been telling yourself you're the agent who hopes the commission question never comes up. That's the old story, and it's the one the settlement was built to punish. The agents keeping their rate think of themselves as the person who names their worth before anyone asks. You just wrote the line that does it. That's not bragging. That's the job the buyer's paying for, finally said out loud.

TIM: Go write the line.

D.J.: They came for the commission and found out it was never the number. It was whether you could say it. We'll see you next week.

END
